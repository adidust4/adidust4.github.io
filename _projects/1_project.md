Early childhood is the most critical stage for brain development. Repeated social interactions between infants and mothers, such as touching, mutual gazing, and vocalizations, are critical for the infant's later emotional, cognitive and physical development. A lack of positive interactions, which can lead to neglect, has a negative impact on cognitive and social-emotional development.

The Children’s Hospital of Los Angeles’ (CHLA) Levitt Lab conducts a ‘Family First’ research study, collecting various longitudinal and multimodal information about mother and infant dyads. The Interaction Lab collaborates on this project using multimodal machine learning techniques to assess how early life stressors affect measures of resilience in children. Understanding the presence of resilience in children with early life stress (ELS) through machine learning is crucial to comprehensively analyze and interpret the multimodal data collected. Classical statistical methods, while useful, have limitations in accuracy and predictive power. We enhance classical statistical approaches by integrating machine learning techniques to distinguish between children who show resilience to ELS and those who do not.
